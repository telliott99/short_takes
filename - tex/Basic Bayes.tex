Bayes rule (or theorem, law) and its application is a huge subject.  Here we only scratch the surface.  

It's about conditional probability, where our interest is in whether something, an event ($E$), is true \emph{given} that we know something else ($F$) is true or has happened.
\[ p(E | F) \]
For example, you may take a rapid test for cancer $C$ and receive a positive result $+$.  The question you will ask is what is the probability that I have cancer \emph{given} that positive result.
\[ p(C | +) \]

A very common response is to give the probability that a person with cancer will have a positive result $p(+ | C)$.  But this is not what we asked, and although it is relevant, it cannot give us the whole of what we want to know.

\subsection*{table}

The usual characterization of a test is by its error rates.  There are two types of error:  first is a false negative, $FN$, which for this example is the occurrence of a negative result when cancer is present.  The second is a false positive, $FP$, a positive result when the subject does not actually have cancer.

The other possible results are sometimes called $TP$ (true positive) and $TN$ (true negative).  It's easy to make a table:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ |c|c|c| } 
 \hline
   & C & no C \\ 
 + & TP & FP  \\ 
 - & FN & TN  \\  
 \hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Two descriptors are used for these error rates, which are unfortunately, easily confused.  \emph{Sensitivity} is the ability to correctly identify persons \emph{with} disease.  High sensitivity means a low rate of $FN$.  A very sensitive test is good at finding cases.

\emph{Specificity} is the ability to correctly identify persons \emph{without} disease.  High specificity means a low rate of $FP$.  A very specific test does not give many false positives.  Both qualities are obviously desirable.

For example, a very good test might have a $FN$ rate of 0.01 and a $FP$ rate of 0.05.  The other values are 1 minus those.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ |c|c|c| } 
 \hline
   & C & no C \\ 
 + & 0.99 & 0.05  \\ 
 - & 0.01 & 0.95  \\  
  total & 1.00 & 1.00  \\ 
 \hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

While this is a nice visual grouping of the error rates, it is not enough.  It is not true, for example, that for people with $+$ test result can be assigned to the $C$ and no $C$ groups in that proportion.  

This would only be true if just as many people had cancer as were cancer-free.  And of course, there is the problem that these "rates" don't add up to $1$.

The other piece of data we need is the prevalence of cancer in the population that is tested.  The easiest way to see the math work out is to use a large round number for the total tested, say, 10,000.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ |c|c|c|c| } 
 \hline
   & C & no C &  \\ 
 + & 0.99 & 0.05 & --- \\ 
 - & 0.01 & 0.95 & --- \\ 
 &  &  & 10000 \\ 
 \hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Suppose the prevalence is 2\%.  What we have, then, is a population consisting of 200 persons with cancer and the rest without.  We leave the internal cells blank for the moment, because we are going to shift from proportions to total numbers.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ |c|c|c|c| } 
 \hline
   & C & no C &  \\ 
 + & --- & --- & --- \\ 
 - & --- & --- & --- \\ 
 total & 200 & 9800 & 10000 \\ 
 \hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Multiply the total for each of the two groups by the proportions that we had above (the error rates).  I obtain
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ |c|c|c|c| } 
 \hline
   & C & no C &  \\ 
 + & 198 & 490 & --- \\ 
 - & 2 & 9310 & --- \\ 
 total & 200 & 9800 & 10000 \\ 
 \hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

We can, if we wish, fill in the marginal totals by addition.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ |c|c|c|c| } 
 \hline
   & C & no C &  total \\ 
 + & 198 & 490 & 688 \\ 
 - & 2 & 9310 & 9312 \\ 
 total & 200 & 9800 & 10000 \\ 
 \hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

There are two conclusions.  The first is not surprising and even reassuring, if your result is negative --- there is almost no chance that you have cancer.  The test is very specific.

However, the second is that, of 688 people with a positive test result, only 198 or about 2 in 7 = 29\% of them have cancer.

That is
\[ p(+ | C) = 0.99 \]
but
\[ p(C | +) = 0.29 \]

The reason is that even though the test is very sensitive, the number of cases actually present is low.  This often feels counter-intuitive.

\subsection*{Bayes}

Bayes theorem is a rule to tell us the probability that someone both has cancer and has a positive test result.  It requires knowledge of $FN$, $FP$ and the prevalence.

A general result from the laws of probability is that
\[ p(E \cap F) = p(E|F) \ p(F) = (F|E) \ p(E) \]
$\cap$ means AND.  The probability of two events is equal to the probability of one times the \emph{conditional} probability of the second, given the first.  It works in both directions.

This can be rearranged to give
\[ p(E|F) = \frac{p(F|E) \ p(E)}{p(F)} \]

using the symbols from our example
\[ p(C \cap +) = p(C|+) \ p(+) = p(+|C) \ p(C) \]
thus
\[ p(C|+) = \frac{p(+|C) \ p(C)}{p(+)} \]

The first term in the numerator on the right-hand side is $1- FN$ = 0.99.  The probability of cancer is the prevalence $p(C)$, which we said is 2\% or 0.02.  This is often taken as the prevalence in the general population, although in reality it should be the prevalence in people who have been administered a test.

The third is the fraction of all tests which are positive, which is 0.69.  This is the part of the calculation that is a bit more complicated, it is 198 + 490 = 688 divided by $N$.

After multiplying it all out, I get 29\%, as expected.

There is a slightly easier way to do the calculation, which also makes clear how the Bayes approach reflects the way we think about science.

\subsection*{odds form of Bayes}

First, we must explain the relationship between probability and odds.

Suppose we have the probability book's proverbial urn, which is a huge opaque jar.  Suppose that it contains 10 red ($r$) balls and 90 white ($w$) ones.  We can call the event of drawing a red ball $R$, and its probability is $p(R) = 0.10$.  That is
\[ p(R) = \frac{r}{r + w} \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \  \ \  p(W) = \frac{w}{r + w}  = 1 - p(R) \]

Odds are the ratio between two possible choices or events.  They are normally given in whole numbers so here we would say that the odds \emph{against} the less likely possibility are 9 to 1.
\[ o(W) = \frac{90}{10} =  \frac{w}{r} = \frac{p(W)}{p(R)}  \]

Odds are very commonly used in horse racing, where for most horses the chance of winning is low and adds against of 4 to 1 or 12 to 1 are usual.

If the probability of some event (my horse winning the 3rd race)  is 10\% or 0.10 then the probability of losing is 0.90 and we expect that for every 90 losses we should have 10 wins so the odds against winning are 9 to 1.

The odds form of Bayes rule is
\[ o(G|E) = \frac{p(E|G)}{p(E|G^{c})} \times o(G) \]

We start with some odds of an event G, called $o(G)$ on the far right.  These odds are updated by multiplication with a factor and the result, on the left, is the updated odds of G, \emph{given} E.  The updating factor is called the \emph{likelihood ratio}.

Based on what we knew before the results of the cancer test, the individual has some odds of $C$.  Normally we express this as a whole number for the more common result, namely

\[ o(C^c) = 49 \text{ to } 1 \]

The symbol $C^c$ means the case where $C$ is not true (not present).  The $c$ stands for \emph{complement}.

The odds $o(C^c)$ of not having cancer before we receive any data, are 49 to 1.  As a probability $p(C^c) = 1 - p(C)$.  Given as odds, $o(C^c) = (1 - p(C))/p(C)$.

All this is just from the prevalence.  

These odds are updated based on a ratio of probabilities.  Let us use rewrite what we had before
\[ o(G|E) = \frac{p(E|G)}{p(E|G^{c})} \times o(G) \]

It is a little tricky because $C^c$ is the common outcome.  With our specific symbols:
\[ o(C^c|+) = \frac{p(+|C^c)}{p(+|C)} \times o(C^c) \]
  
The numerator $p(+|C^c)$ is the false positive rate $FP$, the probability that a person without cancer will test positive, which was 5\% or 0.05.

$p(+|C)$ is the probability that a person with cancer will test positive, which as we said is 1 minus $FN$ or 0.99.  The likelihood ratio is 5/99. or about 1/20.  

Update the prior odds of 49 to 1 by dividing by 20 to obtain about 2-1/2 to 1 or 5 to 2.  These are the new odds that one has cancer, based on a positive screening result.  It is still more probable that we are cancer-free than not.

The ratio of probabilities may take a minute to think through, but the logic of the equation is just like what we suppose happens in the scientific enterprise.  We start with some prior belief about the hypothesis, and then update that belief on the basis of new information.

Furthermore, if we restrict ourself to this particular problem (test for a rare condition), let's look at the middle term.  We had
\[ \frac{p(+|C^c)}{p(+|C)} \]
This is just
\[ \frac{FP}{1 - FN} \]

So, before the test we know the prevalence, from which we have odds $o(C)$ and $o(C^c)$.  We compute $FP/(1-FN)$ and multiply.  Done.  We have the new odds for $o(C^c)$ given the test result.

If the whole business with the complement $C^c$ is confusing, we can also allow odds that are less than $1$, and just invert everything, since $o(E) = 1/o(E^c)$.  

Hence it is also the case that
\[ o(C|+) = \frac{p(+|C)}{p(+|C^c)} \times o(C) \]

The prior odds $o(C)$ before the test were 1 to 49, we multiply by 20 to obtain posterior odds of 1 to 2.5.  That is 2.5 chances for no cancer for every 1 to have cancer or a probability of 1/3.5 = 29\%.

It is even more natural to do these calculations in terms of logarithms, where updating a prior is simple addition.  In that case we can talk about the \emph{log odds}.
