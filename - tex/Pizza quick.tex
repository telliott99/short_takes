\subsection*{problem}

I found this in Acheson's \emph{The Wonder Book of Geometry}.  It is called the ``pizza theorem".  
\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.65] {Acheson_G111.png} \end{center}

Consider a circular pizza pie.  Choose a point \emph{anywhere} in the pie.  Draw two perpendicular chords crossing at the point, with any orientation, and then fill in with two more chords that bisect the angles

Form the sum of the areas of alternate slices.  Above, the two collections are shaded to tell them apart.

The total dark area and the total light area are always equal.  The pizza is evenly divided, even though the slices are wonky.

\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pizza_theorem}

\subsection*{solution}

We will refer to the point where all the chords cross as the \emph{grid center}.  It is clear that if the grid center coincides with the circle center, then by the radial symmetry of the circle and the equal angles, all the segments are the same.  The equal area result is obviously correct.
\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.35] {pizza10.png} \end{center}
For the drawings, I've chosen to mark the ``shaded" slices with dots.  

We will show that any movement parallel to a chord of the grid, whether it is along a diagonal of the circle or not, conserves the equal apportionment of areas between shaded and unshaded regions.  The key movement is shown in the figure (to the right):
\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.35] {pizza4b.png} \end{center}
``Shaded" regions are marked with Roman numerals.  Consider the increase in shaded area due to this movement.  The increase comes from rectangles formed by arms of three chords:  $c$, $b$ and $h$.  The decrease in shaded area is due to rectangles on the other end of the same chords:  $a$, $d$ and $g$.  The net change in area is just
\[ \Delta A = \sqrt{2}(b - a) + (c - d) + (h - g) \]
The chord $ab$ counts more because it is perpendicular to the direction of travel, so it captures more area.

Our hypothesis is that $\Delta A = 0$ so we should have that
\[ \sqrt{2}(a - b) = (c - d) + (h - g) \]

We'll call that the \emph{invariant}.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pizza7.png} \end{center}

We can find the distance that the center of any chord lies from the grid center by taking the difference between the long and the short arm and dividing by $2$.  Call that distance $\delta$.  Dividing both sides of the invariant by $2$ gives
\[ \sqrt{2} \ \delta_{ab} = \delta_{cd} + \delta_{gh} \]

This invariant clearly holds when the grid center remains on a diagonal of the circle (vertical movement).

The red lines represent the lines from the centers of the chords to the center of the circle (red dot).  These are perpendicular bisectors of the angled chords.  Because of the right angles, these are also equal to $\delta$ as defined above.

The vertical down from the red dot (center of the circle) to the grid center is $\delta_{ab}$, since the center of this chord coincides with the center of the circle.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pizza7.png} \end{center}

It is clear that for this position
\[ \sqrt{2} \ \delta_{ab} = \delta_{cd} + \delta_{gh} \]

Horizontal movement doesn't change $\delta_{ab}$.
\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pizza12.png} \end{center}
It \emph{does} change $\delta_{cd}$ and $\delta_{gh}$, but they are adjacent sides in a rectangle whose four vertices are the chord centers, the circle center and the grid center.

\begin{center} \includegraphics [scale=0.4] {pizza9.png} \end{center}
The total length of the two red lines doesn't change with horizontal motion, so neither does the total of the other sides of the rectangle, which is exactly 
$\delta_{cd} + \delta_{gh}$.  Our invariant is really invariant!

$\square$

\subsection*{details}

$\circ$ \ Vertical motion along a diagonal doesn't change the allocation of areas, because that diagonal remains an axis of symmetry, where each shaded region has an identical unshaded region across the axis from it.

$\circ$ \ It is possible to reach any position and orientation by vertical plus horizontal movement.  (\emph{Proof}:  start with any position and perform a horizontal translation followed by vertical movement along a diagonal of the circle.  Now reverse the motion.)

$\circ$ \ The curved shapes at the ends of each chord, resulting from the motion, are mirror images.  Since one changes the shaded and the other the unshaded area, they cancel.
